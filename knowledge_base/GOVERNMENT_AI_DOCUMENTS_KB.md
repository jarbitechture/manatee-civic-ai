1. Bignami (2022)

Francesca Bignami
The American Journal of Comparative Law, Vol. 70, Supplement 1, pp. i312-i346, 2022
Artificial Intelligence Accountability of Public Administration
Francesca Bignami
The American Journal of Comparative Law, Vol. 70, Supplement 1, pp. i312-i346, 2022
Overview
Francesca Bignami, a professor at George Washington University Law School, examines the use and regulation of artificial
intelligence in U.S. administrative agencies. The article was prepared as a reply to the questionnaire circulated in advance of
the 2022 International Congress of Comparative Law, providing a comparative perspective on AI accountability in public
administration.
Key Arguments
Bignami argues that as AI systems become more prevalent in government decision-making, traditional mechanisms of
administrative accountability face significant challenges. The opacity of algorithmic systems complicates established legal
doctrines around due process, notice, and explanation that have historically governed administrative action.
- The article canvasses the current landscape of AI deployment in U.S. federal agencies, documenting how
automated decision-making systems are used in areas such as benefits determination, regulatory enforcement, and
risk assessment.
- Bignami examines the legal frameworks available to hold agencies accountable when they use AI, including the
Administrative Procedure Act (APA), constitutional due process requirements, and emerging statutory and regulatory
mechanisms.
- The article identifies significant gaps in existing accountability frameworks, particularly around transparency,
explainability, and meaningful review of algorithmic decisions.
- The comparative law perspective reveals both similarities and differences in how civil law and common law
jurisdictions approach AI accountability in government.
Methodological Approach
The article employs a comparative legal methodology, analyzing U.S. administrative law alongside international approaches.
Bignami draws on regulatory analysis, case law examination, and institutional analysis of agency practices to build her
argument about accountability gaps.
Key Findings and Implications
The article concludes that existing accountability mechanisms are insufficient for the challenges posed by AI in government,
and calls for new legal tools that address the unique characteristics of algorithmic decision-making, including requirements for
transparency, audit, and meaningful human oversight. The comparative perspective underscores the importance of adapting
accountability frameworks to the specific legal and institutional contexts of different jurisdictions.

Yu-Che Chen, Michael J. Ahn, and Yi-Fan Wang
Sustainability, Vol. 15, No. 6, Article 4796, 2023
Overview
Chen, Ahn, and Wang investigate the relationship between public sector AI deployment and its impact on public values,
combining a systematic literature review with an original national survey of U.S. government employees. The article was
published in MDPI Sustainability in March 2023.
Research Design
The study employs a mixed-methods approach:
- Systematic Literature Review: Analyzes existing scholarship on AI and public values to identify key themes, impacts,
governance challenges, and proposed solutions.
- National Survey: Surveys U.S. government employees to capture practitioner perspectives on AI governance and
public values, providing empirical grounding to the literature review findings.
Key Findings
- Broad inclusion of diverse public values is necessary when considering AI governance - not just efficiency, but also
fairness, transparency, privacy, equity, and democratic participation.
- Transparency emerges as the most salient concern across multiple governance challenges, including transparency
of algorithms, data use, and decision-making processes.
- Stakeholder participation and collaboration are identified as the most important governance solutions, emphasizing
the need for inclusive decision-making about AI deployment.
- Government employees express both optimism about AI potential and concern about governance gaps, particularly
around accountability and bias.
Theoretical Contribution
The article bridges the gap between theoretical discussions of public values and practical AI governance, arguing that public
administration scholarship must engage more deeply with the value implications of AI. It provides a framework for
understanding how different public values are affected by AI deployment and how governance mechanisms can protect these
values.
Institutional Context

Michael J. Ahn is at the John W. McCormack Graduate School, University of Massachusetts Boston.

4. Kawakami et al. (2024)
Studying Up Public Sector AI: How Networks of Power Relations
Anna Kawakami and colleagues
Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction, Vol. 8, CSCW2, November 2024
Studying Up Public Sector AI: How Networks of Power Relations
Anna Kawakami and colleagues
Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction, Vol. 8, CSCW2, November 2024
Overview
This paper, published at CSCW 2024, examines how public sector agencies make decisions about AI tool adoption and
design. Borrowing from the anthropological practice of "studying up," the research focuses on those in positions of power and
responsibility - the decision-makers who shape how AI tools are developed and deployed in high-stakes government contexts.
Research Design
The study employs qualitative methods:
- Semi-structured interviews and design activities with 16 agency decision-makers from 19 public sector agencies
involved in human services (e.g., child welfare, predictive policing).
- The "studying up" approach reorients research from studying affected communities to examining those who hold
decision-making power about AI systems.
- Analysis of how decisions are influenced by interactions with and assumptions about other actors, including frontline
workers, agency leaders, legal systems, contracted companies, and impacted communities.
Key Findings
- Networks of power relations significantly shape how agencies adopt and design AI tools. Decision-making does not
occur in isolation but is influenced by multiple stakeholders with competing interests and unequal power.
- Key conflicts were identified among stakeholders - for example, frontline workers wanted to be involved in
decision-making earlier, but agency leaders and R&D experts viewed earlier involvement as impractical.
- There are no formal policies in most public agencies requiring the involvement and consultation of frontline workers
in AI adoption decisions. As a result, frontline workers have little decision-making power about AI systems that
directly affect their work processes.
- Contracted companies and technology vendors exert significant influence over AI design decisions, often without
adequate accountability to affected communities.
Implications
The paper argues for structural reforms that give frontline workers and affected communities meaningful voice in AI design and
adoption decisions, rather than relying on ad hoc or informal consultation. It calls attention to the disconnect between
aspirational AI governance principles and the reality of how AI decisions are made in government agencies.
Additional 2024 Publications
Kawakami also published "Do Responsible AI Artifacts Advance Stakeholder Goals? Four Key Barriers Perceived by Legal
and Civil Stakeholders" (AIES 2024), examining how responsible AI tools and artifacts often fail to serve the needs of legal and
civil society stakeholders, and a policy memo with the Federation of American Scientists on expanding state and local
government capacity for responsible AI procurement.

6. Miller (2022)
Explanation in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Social Sciences
Tim Miller
Artificial Intelligence, Vol. 267, pp. 1-38 (foundational work; updated perspectives 2022), 2019/2022
Explanation in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Social Sciences
Tim Miller
Artificial Intelligence, Vol. 267, pp. 1-38 (foundational work; updated perspectives 2022), 2019/2022
Overview
Tim Miller, a professor at the University of Queensland (previously University of Melbourne), produced one of the most
influential works on explainable AI (XAI) by arguing that the field should build on existing research from philosophy, cognitive
psychology, and social psychology rather than relying solely on technical intuitions about what constitutes a good explanation.
His continued work through 2022 has expanded and refined these arguments in the context of rapidly evolving AI systems.
Core Argument
Miller contends that there has been a fundamental disconnect in the XAI field: most work in explainable AI uses only
researchers' intuitions about what constitutes a "good" explanation, despite vast and valuable bodies of research in philosophy,
psychology, and cognitive science on how people actually define, generate, select, evaluate, and present explanations.
Key Contributions
- Social Science of Explanation: Reviews decades of research from social psychology and cognitive science on how
humans explain events to one another, identifying principles that should guide XAI design.
- Contrastive Explanations: Demonstrates that people do not ask "Why did X happen?" but rather "Why did X happen
instead of Y?" - explanations are fundamentally contrastive, and XAI systems should generate contrastive
explanations.
- Selectivity: People are selective about what causes they cite in explanations, preferring abnormal or unexpected
causes over necessary conditions. XAI systems should similarly be selective rather than exhaustive.
- Social Process: Explanation is fundamentally a social and interactive process, not merely a product. This has
implications for how XAI interfaces should be designed - as dialogues rather than one-shot outputs.
Relevance to AI Governance
Miller's work has direct implications for AI governance and accountability frameworks:
- The right to explanation enshrined in frameworks like the AI Bill of Rights and the EU AI Act requires AI systems to
provide meaningful explanations - Miller's work defines what "meaningful" should look like based on how humans
actually process explanations.
- Accountability mechanisms for public sector AI require that algorithmic decisions be explainable to affected
individuals, auditors, and oversight bodies - each requiring different types of explanations.
- The work underscores that technical explainability metrics alone are insufficient; explanations must be evaluated
based on whether they are useful and understandable to their intended audience.
Impact and Citations
Miller's work has become one of the most cited papers in the XAI field, serving as a foundational reference for human-centered
approaches to explainable AI. It has influenced the development of explanation standards in multiple governance frameworks
and regulatory proposals worldwide.

7. Comparative Analysis and Thematic Synthesis
The six works reviewed here collectively address the governance, accountability, and implementation of AI in public sector
contexts. Several cross-cutting themes emerge from this body of scholarship:
Theme 1: Accountability Gaps
Both Bignami (2022) and Kawakami et al. (2024) identify significant gaps between existing accountability mechanisms and the
challenges posed by AI in government. Bignami focuses on legal frameworks (APA, due process), while Kawakami examines
organizational power dynamics that undermine meaningful accountability in practice.
Theme 2: The Centrality of Values and Ethics
Chen et al. (2023) and Merhi (2023) converge on the importance of values and ethics in AI governance and implementation.
Chen's survey of government employees reveals transparency as the paramount concern, while Merhi's analytical hierarchy
process identifies ethics as the single most critical success factor for AI implementation.
Theme 3: Power, Bias, and Equity
Hanna et al. (2024) and Kawakami et al. (2024) both examine how power dynamics shape AI outcomes. Hanna focuses on
how data practices embed racial, gender, and class biases, while Kawakami reveals how organizational power relations
exclude frontline workers and affected communities from AI design decisions.
Theme 4: The Human Dimension
Miller (2022) and Chen et al. (2023) emphasize that AI governance must be fundamentally human-centered. Miller argues that
explanations must align with how humans actually think and communicate, not with technical metrics. Chen documents that
government employees value stakeholder participation and collaboration as the most important governance mechanisms.
Theme 5: Implementation Challenges
Merhi (2023) provides the most structured analysis of implementation factors, but all six works acknowledge the difficulty of
translating governance principles into practice. Kawakami shows this gap concretely in government agencies, Bignami
identifies it in legal frameworks, and Hanna documents it in data practices.
Collectively, these works argue that effective AI governance in the public sector requires: (1) reformed legal and institutional
accountability mechanisms, (2) meaningful inclusion of diverse stakeholders, (3) rigorous attention to bias and equity, (4)
human-centered approaches to transparency and explanation, and (5) holistic implementation strategies that address
technological, organizational, environmental, and ethical dimensions simultaneously.

Generative AI Guidelines 
Last updated: April 24, 2025 
 
Executive Summary: 
Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a branch of AI technology that interacts with user-
provided creativity to generate content—such as memos, letters, proposals, images, voice, and 
music— at the request of a user. Many organizations have banned Generative AI, while others 
allow unrestricted usage. The City of San José (City) recognizes the opportunity for a controlled, 
ethical, and responsible approach to use AI that acknowledges the benefits to efficiency while 
mitigating the risks around AI bias, privacy, and cybersecurity.   
Previous iterations of the Generative AI Guidelines were released prior to the creation of a City-
wide AI Policy. Recognizing the collaborative work between City staff, San José residents, the 
Digital Privacy Advisory Task Force, and countless partner government agencies in the GovAI 
Coalition, the City published its AI policy (City Policy Manual 1.7.12), governing these guidelines.   
At a baseline, users must follow these rules while using Generative AI for City work or using City 
resources, including direct services like ChatGPT, Zoom AI, and Copilot and extensions like 
Compose.ai: 
1. Information entered into Generative AI systems may be subject to a Public Records Act 
(PRA) request, may be viewable and usable by the company, and may be leaked 
unencrypted in a data breach. Unless using a City-issued Generative AI tool, users are 
prohibited from using any prompts that may include information not suitable for public 
release (such as confidential or personally identifiable information).  
2. Review, revise, and fact check via multiple sources any content created from a Generative 
AI system as these systems can make mistakes. Users are responsible for the accuracy 
and appropriateness of material created with AI assistance.  
3. Limit the environmental impact of your Generative AI usage by using search engines for 
general questions, using fewer prompts where feasible, and putting boundaries on

City of San José Generative AI Guidelines               April 24, 2025 

Definitions 
User: Staff, contractors, interns/fellows, volunteers, or others using Generative AI for City work 
purposes. 
City: The city government of San José located in California, United States of America. 
Generative AI: A machine that automatically creates content such as text, audio, or image. 
Artificial Intelligence (AI): Machines doing tasks that typically require human intelligence. 
Machine Learning: A type of AI in which computers use data to “learn” tasks through algorithms. 
Algorithm: A set of steps or procedure designed to perform a task, such as mathematical 
operations (e.g., addition) or logical rules, to solve a problem or perform a task. 
 
Purpose of Guidelines 
“Generative AI,” such as ChatGPT, grew from a niche topic to a variety of publicly available tools 
with hundreds of millions of adopters in less than one year. Among other things, Generative AI 
presents an incredible opportunity for people to increase their efficiency and efficacy in work. 
Generative AI has also been used for several irresponsible applications including faking news 
headlines,1 leaking personal information,2 and enabling phishing cyber-attacks.3 
The City is actively working to create policies and procedures around AI in general. This 
document serves as part of an evolving governance structure around responsible AI usage.  
Application of the Guidelines 
This document applies to all use of Generative AI by a City staff member, contractor, volunteer, 
or other person while performing a role for the City (collectively “users”). This document does 
not apply to users of Generative AI for personal purposes or business purposes unassociated 
with the City.  
Generative AI does not refer to algorithms that a person directly defines. For example, a 
spreadsheet a human created to calculates taxes owed based on income is not “Generative AI.” 
A general rule is that if you cannot write the system’s entire algorithm, either because you do 
not understand the math or because it would take years to write down, then it is probably AI.

Assessing Risk in Generative AI Use Cases 
The risk presented by Generative AI tools varies by use case, with the risk spectrum ranging from 
mid-risk to high-risk to intolerable risk.  
Generative AI risk is determined by two key factors: 
17. Risk of information breach: the potential harm if the information exchanged with a

information. In general, if Generative AI is used in relation to City processes that can alter

thoroughly reviewed by multiple users before any document is finalized or action is taken.

Details for Understanding Generative AI Risk  
Understanding “Risk of Information Breach”  
General rule: If the information exchanged with a Generative AI system would be harmful to a 
person or community if made public, it is a high or intolerable risk. Services like ChatGPT have 
been compromised in the past and leaked personal information.17 Until private applications with 
higher security are deployed in the City, all information exchanged with Generative AI has a 
reasonable risk of being compromised. 
Low-risk information includes non-identifying and non-confidential information. For example, a 
simple email response or instructive documents often contain only general information that 
would not present any risk if made public. 
Mid-risk information includes personally identifiable information (e.g., full name, birth date, 
email address) and confidential business information that may have larger implications to City 
processes. Until a private application is deployed with security measures approved by the 
Cybersecurity Office, no high-risk information shall be provided to a Generative AI system. 
High-risk information includes highly sensitive and identifying information. This includes data 
such as credit card numbers, bank account information, social security numbers, and other 
information that requires rigorous security measures and compliance standards before being 
processed.  
Understanding “Risk of Adverse Impact” 
General rule: If you are using Generative AI in relation to City processes that can alter an 
individual or community’s rights, freedoms, or access to services, it is at least high risk and should 
be thoroughly reviewed before any document is finalized or action is taken. Additionally, any 
action that could reasonably lead to the City engaging in legal infringements on intellectual 
property are prohibited.

Be Aware of Targeted Cyber Attacks Using Generative AI 
Although City staff are already familiar with handling cyber risks like phishing and malware, the 
advent of generative AI introduces heightened cybersecurity risks as the attacks can be more 
complex and personalized. Cyber threat actors may use generative AI in their attacks in the 
following ways: 
• Writing AI-powered, personalized phishing emails: With the help of generative AI, 
phishing emails no longer have the tell-tale signs of a scam—such as poor spelling, bad 
grammar, and lack of context. Plus, with AI like ChatGPT, threat actors can launch 
phishing attacks at unprecedented speed and scale. 
• Generating deep fake data: Since AI can create convincing imitations of human 
activities—like writing, speech, and static and video images. Generative AI can be used in 
fraudulent activities such as identity theft, financial fraud, and disinformation. 
• Cracking CAPTCHAs and password guessing: Used by sites and networks to comb out bots 
seeking unauthorized access, CAPTCHA can now be bypassed by hackers. By utilizing AI, 
they can also fulfill other repetitive tasks such as password guessing and brute-force 
attacks.

with caution. For example, there have been numerous incidents of instructors using ChatGPT 
detection tools falsely accusing students of plagiarism, and endangering their grades and even

3. Current AI Initiatives and Pilots
Peregrine Emergency Management Dashboard
Manatee County was the FIRST local government in the country to use Peregrine's AI-powered technology for emergency
management - a distinction that demonstrates the county's willingness to pioneer responsible AI adoption in critical public
safety applications.
The partnership with tech startup Peregrine began in August 2024, just before Hurricane Helene. The AI-powered dashboard
integrates diverse data streams including river gauges, pump station locations, flooding reports, and damage assessments into
a comprehensive real-time operational picture.
Results and Impact
- During Hurricanes Debby, Helene, and Milton (2024), the Peregrine dashboard enabled faster damage assessment,
helping the county secure major disaster declaration status faster than any previous hurricane.
- The county's "GIS Room" features six monitors displaying real-time information, enabling coordinated emergency
response across all departments.
- The county is actively working with Peregrine to launch an additional dashboard displaying flooding reports from 911
calls screened and transcribed by AI - extending AI capabilities into real-time emergency triage.
- The three hurricanes caused over $750 million in property damage across the county, and faster access to recovery
funds was directly attributed to the AI-enabled damage assessment.
State AI Efficiency Pilot Program
In December 2025, Manatee County commissioners reviewed a proposal to participate in a state-led AI pilot program through
the Florida Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) initiative. Key details:
- Proposed partner: Polytech, a firm with prior public-sector experience and tools for analyzing large datasets.
- Scope: Analysis of county data (approximately 51 gigabytes) to identify efficiency improvement opportunities.
- Cost: Estimated $2,000-$4,000 depending on data volume.
- Timeline: Actionable insights within approximately 90 days.
- Status: Commission gave staff directional approval to proceed with collecting additional information. Commissioners
urged thorough security checks before any contract approval.

4. Florida State AI Policy Landscape
Governor DeSantis AI Bill of Rights Proposal
In late 2025, Governor DeSantis announced a "Citizen Bill of Rights for Artificial Intelligence" as a priority for the 2026
legislative session. Key provisions include:
- Prohibiting AI from using an individual's name, image, or likeness without permission.
- Protection from deepfakes and AI-generated impersonation.
- Parental controls for minors accessing AI platforms, including notification of concerning behavior.
- Prohibition of AI-provided therapy or mental health counseling.
- Ban on DeepSeek and other Chinese-created AI tools in state and local government agencies.
- Data center infrastructure requirements including utility cost protections and siting restrictions.
SB 482: AI Consumer Protection
Filed by Senator Tom Leek (R-Ormond Beach), SB 482 would codify AI consumer protections in Florida law, including
measures to protect minors from harmful AI-generated content and require transparency from AI service providers.
Federal-State Tension
Florida's AI regulatory approach exists in tension with the Trump Administration's executive order seeking to preempt state AI
laws. Governor DeSantis has asserted Florida's right to regulate AI independently, stating that he would consider any
executive order overriding state laws to be unlawful. This creates a dynamic policy environment that Manatee County must
navigate.
Implication for Manatee County: The evolving state and federal AI landscape makes it important for the county to develop
flexible AI governance policies that can adapt to new requirements while maintaining alignment with both state and federal
frameworks.

1. Overview & Purpose
The NIST Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) was released on January 26, 2023, as publication
NIST AI 100-1. It provides a voluntary, rights-preserving, non-sector-specific framework for improving the ability to incorporate
trustworthiness considerations into the design, development, use, and evaluation of AI products, services, and systems.
The AI RMF was developed through an open, transparent, consensus-driven process involving extensive stakeholder
engagement, including multiple workshops, public comments, and input from industry, academia, civil society, and government
agencies.
The framework is intended for use by AI actors across the AI lifecycle, including those who design, develop, deploy, evaluate,
and use AI systems or provide the data and infrastructure for these activities. It is designed to be adaptable to all types and
sizes of organizations.
Key Objectives
- Offer a resource to organizations designing, developing, deploying, or using AI systems to help manage the many
risks of AI.
- Improve the ability to incorporate trustworthiness considerations into the design, development, use, and evaluation
of AI.
- Be adaptable to the broadest possible set of AI technologies and use contexts, applicable across sectors.
- Complement and be consistent with other NIST frameworks, including the Cybersecurity Framework and Privacy
Framework.
Structure
The AI RMF is organized into two main parts: Part 1 discusses how organizations can frame the risks related to AI, and the
characteristics of trustworthy AI systems. Part 2 comprises the core of the framework, with four functions (Govern, Map,
Measure, Manage) broken down into categories and subcategories providing outcome-based guidance.

2. Trustworthy AI Characteristics
The AI RMF identifies seven key characteristics of trustworthy AI systems. These characteristics are sociotechnical in nature,
reflecting both technical and human considerations:
1. Valid and Reliable
Validation is the confirmation that AI system requirements and performance objectives are fit for the intended use. Reliability
means the system can perform as required, without failure, for a given time interval under given conditions. Systems should be
tested, monitored, and documented throughout their lifecycle.
2. Safe
AI systems should not endanger human life, health, property, or the environment under defined conditions. Safety includes the
ability of a system to maintain or restore a safe state when problems arise and to mitigate the consequences of unsafe
behavior.
3. Secure and Resilient
AI systems should be able to maintain confidentiality, integrity, and availability through protection mechanisms that prevent
unauthorized access and use. Resilience refers to the ability to withstand unexpected adverse events or changes, including
adversarial attacks.
4. Accountable and Transparent
Accountability presupposes transparency. Transparency reflects the extent to which information about an AI system and its
outputs is available to individuals interacting with the system. Higher levels of risk tolerance generally require higher levels of
transparency.
5. Explainable and Interpretable
Explainability refers to a representation of the reasons or causes that led an AI system to generate particular outputs.
Interpretability refers to the meaning of AI system output in the context of its designed functional purposes.
6. Privacy-Enhanced
Privacy values such as anonymity, confidentiality, and control generally should guide choices for AI system design,
development, and deployment. Privacy-enhancing technologies can help address privacy-related risks.
7. Fair - with Harmful Bias Managed
Fairness in AI includes concerns about equality and equity by addressing issues such as harmful bias and discrimination.
Standards of fairness can be complex and contextual, and may not be universally shared.

3. Core Function 1: GOVERN
The GOVERN function establishes and maintains the organizational structures, policies, processes, and practices for AI risk
management. Unlike Map, Measure, and Manage, GOVERN is cross-cutting and applies across all stages of the AI lifecycle.
GOVERN Categories
GOVERN 1: Policies and Procedures
Policies, processes, procedures, and practices across the organization related to the mapping, measuring, and managing of AI
risks are in place, transparent, and implemented effectively. This includes establishing organizational AI risk management
policies and ensuring they are communicated and enforced.
GOVERN 2: Accountability Structures
Accountability structures are in place so that the appropriate teams and individuals are empowered, responsible, and trained
for mapping, measuring, and managing AI risks. Clear lines of authority and responsibility must be defined.
GOVERN 3: Workforce Diversity and AI Expertise
Workforce diversity, equity, inclusion, and accessibility processes are prioritized in the mapping, measuring, and managing of
AI risks throughout the lifecycle. Teams should include diverse perspectives and domain expertise.
GOVERN 4: Organizational Commitments
Organizational teams are committed to a culture that considers and communicates AI risk. Risk management is integrated into
organizational culture, not treated as an afterthought.
GOVERN 5: Processes for Engagement
Processes are in place for robust engagement with relevant AI actors. Regular organizational risk-related engagement with
interested parties and relevant AI actors exists throughout the lifecycle. This includes engagement with affected communities
and stakeholders.
GOVERN 6: Policies for Third-Party AI
Policies and procedures are in place to address AI risks and benefits arising from third-party software and data. Supply chain
risk management practices should extend to AI components, models, and data from external sources.

4. Core Function 2: MAP
The MAP function establishes the context to frame risks related to an AI system. It is concerned with understanding the
environment, intended purposes, and potential impacts of AI systems, forming the foundation for subsequent risk
measurement and management.
MAP Categories
MAP 1: Intended Context of Use
Context is established and understood. The intended purposes, potentially beneficial uses, context-specific laws, norms, and
expectations, and prospective settings in which the AI system will be deployed are understood and documented.
MAP 2: AI System Categorization
Categorization of the AI system is performed. The system is classified based on its characteristics, including its level of
autonomy, its interactions with humans, and the nature and criticality of its intended use.
MAP 3: AI System Benefits and Costs
AI capabilities, targeted usage, goals, and expected benefits and costs compared with appropriate benchmarks are
understood. Cost-benefit analysis should consider both direct and indirect impacts.
MAP 4: Risks and Impacts
Risks and impacts of the AI system are identified and mapped. This includes risks related to bias, security, privacy, and other
harms, as well as risks from third-party components and data dependencies.
MAP 5: Impact Characterization
Impacts to individuals, groups, communities, organizations, and society are characterized. Both positive and negative impacts
should be identified, with particular attention to impacts on vulnerable and marginalized groups.

5. Core Function 3: MEASURE
The MEASURE function employs quantitative, qualitative, or mixed-method tools, techniques, and methodologies to analyze,
assess, benchmark, and monitor AI risk and related impacts. It provides the evidence base for risk management decisions.
MEASURE Categories
MEASURE 1: Appropriate Methods and Metrics
Appropriate methods and metrics are identified and applied. Organizations should select measurement approaches that are fit
for purpose, considering the AI system type, deployment context, and risk profile. Metrics should cover all relevant trustworthy
AI characteristics.
MEASURE 2: AI System Evaluation
AI systems are evaluated for trustworthy characteristics. Each of the seven trustworthiness characteristics (valid and reliable,
safe, secure and resilient, accountable and transparent, explainable and interpretable, privacy-enhanced, and fair) should be
evaluated using appropriate techniques.
MEASURE 3: Risk Tracking
Mechanisms for tracking identified AI risks over time are in place. Continuous monitoring should detect changes in risk profiles,
including model drift, distributional shifts, and evolving threat landscapes.
MEASURE 4: Measurement Effectiveness
Feedback about efficacy of measurement is collected and assessed. Organizations should evaluate whether their
measurement approaches are producing useful and actionable information, and adjust as needed.

6. Core Function 4: MANAGE
The MANAGE function allocates risk resources to mapped and measured risks on a regular basis and as defined by the
GOVERN function. It involves prioritizing, responding to, and recovering from AI risks, and ensuring continuous improvement.
MANAGE Categories
MANAGE 1: Risk Prioritization
AI risks based on assessments and other analytical output from the MAP and MEASURE functions are prioritized, responded
to, and managed. Risk treatment decisions should be documented and communicated to relevant stakeholders.
MANAGE 2: Risk Treatment Strategies
Strategies to maximize AI benefits and minimize negative impacts are planned, prepared, implemented, documented, and
informed by input from relevant AI actors. Strategies may include risk avoidance, mitigation, transfer, or acceptance.
MANAGE 3: Risk Response and Recovery
Plans for minimizing negative impacts of AI systems and maximizing benefits, including mechanisms for response and
recovery when events occur, are in place. Incident response plans should be tested and updated regularly.
MANAGE 4: Residual Risk Documentation
Residual risk of the AI system is documented and acceptable. Residual risk is the risk remaining after risk treatment.
Organizations should clearly document residual risks and obtain appropriate acceptance decisions.

The AI RMF introduces the concept of profiles - selections and prioritizations of the framework outcomes that can be tailored to

Profile Types
- Current Profile: Describes the AI risk management outcomes currently being achieved by the organization.
- Target Profile: Describes the desired AI risk management outcomes the organization seeks to achieve.
- Community Profile: Created by sector-specific or cross-sector groups to address shared AI risk management needs

Released in July 2024, the Generative AI Profile (NIST AI 600-1) is a companion resource that maps unique risks posed by
generative AI systems to the AI RMF subcategories. It identifies 12 unique risks associated with GAI:
- CBRN Information or Capabilities: Eased access to chemical, biological, radiological, or nuclear information.
- Confabulation: Generation of factually inaccurate or fabricated content (hallucinations).
- Data Privacy: Risks to personal information through training data or generated outputs.
- Environmental Impacts: Energy consumption and carbon footprint of large-scale AI systems.
- Human-AI Configuration: Over-reliance on AI, automation bias, and diminished human oversight.
- Information Integrity: Deepfakes, misinformation, and manipulation of information ecosystems.
- Information Security: Novel attack vectors including prompt injection and model poisoning.
- Intellectual Property: Copyright and IP concerns related to training data and generated outputs.
- Obscene, Degrading, or Abusive Content: Generation of harmful or inappropriate content.
- Value Chain and Component Integration: Risks from complex supply chains and third-party components.
- Homogenization: Reduced diversity through convergence on similar model architectures and training data.
- Harmful Bias and Discrimination: Amplification of societal biases through generated content.

1. Overview & Purpose
The Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights is a White House framework published in October 2022 by the Office of Science and
Technology Policy (OSTP). It identifies five principles that should guide the design, use, and deployment of automated
systems to protect the American public in the age of artificial intelligence.
The Blueprint was developed through extensive public engagement, including listening sessions, public comments, and
consultation with experts across government, industry, civil society, and academia. It represents the most comprehensive
federal articulation of rights-based protections for individuals affected by AI systems.
The framework applies to automated systems that have the potential to meaningfully impact the American public's rights,
opportunities, or access to critical resources or services. It is a non-binding guidance document - not legislation or regulation -
that serves as a voluntary framework for responsible AI deployment.
Key Context
The Blueprint was released amid growing concern about AI harms including algorithmic bias in hiring, healthcare, lending, and
criminal justice. It builds on earlier work including the 2020 Executive Order on AI and the NIST AI Risk Management
Framework development process.
The document was developed under the Biden-Harris Administration with leadership from the OSTP, reflecting a
whole-of-government approach to AI governance. It was accompanied by a detailed Technical Companion document providing
specific implementation guidance.

2. Principle 1: Safe and Effective Systems
You should be protected from unsafe or ineffective systems. Automated systems should be developed with consultation from
diverse communities, stakeholders, and domain experts to identify concerns, risks, and potential impacts of the system.
Key Requirements
- Pre-deployment testing: Systems should undergo rigorous testing, including pre-deployment testing, to demonstrate
they are safe and effective. Testing should follow domain-specific best practices.
- Ongoing monitoring: Systems should be subject to continuous monitoring that demonstrates they are safe and
effective based on real-world performance, with mechanisms to address problems as they arise.
- Independent evaluation: Independent evaluation and plain language reporting should be performed in a manner that
is accessible to the public, including affected communities.
- Diverse stakeholder input: Consultation with diverse stakeholders should occur throughout the design, development,
deployment, and maintenance lifecycle.
- Risk identification: Proactive identification of potential risks and harms, including foreseeable misuse, must be part of
the development process.
Scope of Application
This principle applies broadly to all automated systems that could affect public safety, including AI used in healthcare
diagnostics, autonomous vehicles, critical infrastructure management, financial systems, and any domain where system failure
could cause significant harm.

3. Principle 2: Algorithmic Discrimination Protections
You should not face discrimination by algorithms and systems should be used and designed in an equitable way. Algorithmic
discrimination occurs when automated systems contribute to unjustified different treatment or impacts disfavoring people
based on their race, color, ethnicity, sex, religion, age, national origin, disability, veteran status, or genetic information.
Key Requirements
- Proactive equity assessments: Designers, developers, and deployers should perform proactive equity assessments
during the design phase, using representative data and protecting against proxies for demographic features.
- Algorithmic impact assessments: These should include disparity testing to assess whether the system produces
disparate outcomes across protected classes, with clear documentation of findings.
- Accessible reporting: Results of algorithmic impact assessments should be made public whenever possible,
including through plain language notices and explanations.
- Organizational oversight: Entities should ensure there is organizational oversight and clear lines of accountability for
identifying and mitigating algorithmic discrimination.
- Independent auditing: Systems should be subject to independent auditing, especially those used in sensitive
domains such as criminal justice, employment, education, and healthcare.
Protected Classifications
The Blueprint explicitly identifies protected characteristics consistent with existing civil rights frameworks: race, color, ethnicity,
sex (including sexual orientation and gender identity), religion, age, national origin, disability, veteran status, and genetic
information. It also recognizes intersectional discrimination.

4. Principle 3: Data Privacy
You should be protected from abusive data practices via built-in protections and you should have agency over how data about
you is used. Designers, developers, and deployers of automated systems should seek your permission and respect your
decisions regarding collection, use, access, transfer, and deletion of your data.
Key Requirements
- Consent and control: Data collection should be limited to what is strictly necessary, and individuals should have
meaningful control over their data, including the ability to opt out of data collection.
- Built-in protections: Systems should have built-in privacy protections by default, including technical safeguards
against unconsented data use, with privacy-by-design principles.
- Surveillance protections: Continuous surveillance and monitoring technologies should be subject to heightened
scrutiny, and surveillance in sensitive domains (education, housing, healthcare) should be restricted.
- Data minimization: Only data strictly necessary for the specific purpose should be collected, and data should not be
repurposed without explicit consent.
- Ethical review: Where privacy-sensitive data is used, independent ethical review should be conducted, similar to
institutional review boards in research contexts.
Relationship to Existing Law
This principle complements existing data privacy frameworks including HIPAA, FERPA, COPPA, and state-level privacy laws
like the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA). The Blueprint calls for these protections to extend to all automated systems,
not just those covered by sector-specific regulations.

5. Principle 4: Notice and Explanation
You should know that an automated system is being used and understand how and why it contributes to outcomes that impact
you. Designers, developers, and deployers should provide generally accessible plain language documentation including clear
descriptions of the overall system functioning and the role automation plays.
Key Requirements
- Clear notice: Individuals should be informed when an automated system is being used in a way that impacts them,
through timely and accessible notice.
- Plain language explanations: Explanations of how the system works and how it contributed to specific outcomes
should be provided in plain, understandable language - not technical jargon.
- Timely communication: Notice and explanation should be given in a timeframe that allows individuals to address
adverse outcomes, challenge decisions, or seek alternatives.
- Technically valid explanations: Explanations should be technically valid, meaningful, and useful to the individuals
affected, not merely pro forma disclosures.
- Accessible formats: Information should be available in formats accessible to people with disabilities and in multiple

The Blueprint emphasizes that notice and explanation requirements should be calibrated to the significance of the automated
decision. Higher-stakes decisions - such as those affecting employment, credit, housing, healthcare, and education - require

6. Principle 5: Human Alternatives, Consideration, and Fallback
You should be able to opt out, where appropriate, and have access to a person who can quickly consider and remedy
problems you encounter. Automated systems should provide meaningful human alternatives for sensitive or high-stakes
decisions.
Key Requirements
- Opt-out mechanisms: Individuals should be able to opt out of automated systems in favor of a human alternative,

- Human consideration: A human should be available to consider and address problems, errors, or concerns arising
from automated system use, particularly in high-stakes contexts.
- Timely remedies: When automated systems produce errors or cause harm, there should be accessible, timely
mechanisms for individuals to seek and receive remedies.
- Fallback systems: Robust fallback and escalation procedures should be in place so that human decision-makers can

Coordination Gaps

2. Five Guiding Principles for AI
The Georgia Technology Authority has established five guiding principles governing the design, implementation, and
utilization of automated systems. These principles are informed by industry research and experts and serve as a
framework whenever automated systems have significant implications on the rights of Georgians or their access to
essential services.
Principle 1: Implement Responsible Systems
Involve users in the design process, test before launch, and monitor over time. Agencies should ensure that AI systems
are developed with input from stakeholders, thoroughly evaluated prior to deployment, and subject to continuous
oversight to identify and address issues as they arise.
Principle 2: Ensure Fairness and Ethics
Check for bias throughout the AI system lifecycle. This includes assigning specific individuals or teams responsible for
monitoring AI systems for bias and taking corrective actions, with clearly defined accountability ensuring that
bias-related issues are addressed promptly. Agencies must work to prevent discriminatory outcomes and ensure that AI
systems treat all individuals equitably.
Principle 3: Keep AI Use Transparent
Label AI-generated content and provide public documentation. Designers, developers, and deployers of automated
systems should provide plain language documentation that is easily accessible to the public, including clear descriptions
of system functionality and ownership, the role of automation, and explanations of outcomes. Transparency builds public
trust and enables meaningful oversight.
Principle 4: Center Human Involvement
Keep people in control and use AI to support, not replace, human judgment. AI systems should not operate in isolation,
and state agencies must ensure that humans retain control over the operation of AI systems and that human
decision-makers remain responsible for the final decisions made with the support of AI technology. Human oversight is
essential to maintaining accountability.
Principle 5: Maintain Data Quality and Privacy
Establish clear guidelines for data governance to maintain integrity and privacy. Agencies should prioritize robust
security measures and transparent data handling practices while ensuring data accuracy, minimizing storage, and
disposing of obsolete data. Data quality directly affects the reliability and fairness of AI system outputs.

1.1 Overview & Background
The NJ AI Assistant is a powerful generative AI platform that offers a secure sandbox environment for New Jersey State
employees to leverage AI to responsibly improve government services and operations. It was launched in July 2024 by

Assistant to safely offer state employees access to one of the most powerful AI models available by securing
government-friendly terms of service and additional privacy protections.
1.2 Architecture & LLM Wrapper Design
The NJ AI Assistant functions as a secure LLM wrapper -- a custom-built interface layer that sits between state
employees and a commercial large language model. This architectural approach provides several key advantages:
- Custom front-end interface tailored for government workflows and use cases
- Middleware security layer with input/output filtering before prompts reach the underlying LLM
- Hosted entirely on State infrastructure, ensuring data sovereignty
- Government-friendly terms of service negotiated directly with the LLM provider
- No State data is used to train third-party AI models, avoiding putting internal information at risk
The in-house wrapper approach allowed New Jersey to achieve a cost of approximately $1 per user per month,
compared to $20 per user per month for off-the-shelf commercial licenses -- a 95% cost reduction.
1.3 Security & Privacy Protections
The NJ AI Assistant incorporates multiple layers of security and privacy protections:
- Input filters: Prevent the submission of potentially harmful, sensitive, or personally identifiable information
- Output filters: Screen AI-generated responses for harmful, biased, or inappropriate content
- Anti-jailbreak protections: Safeguards to prevent common jailbreak techniques and attempts to circumvent
the model's built-in guardrails
- Data isolation: The tool is hosted on state infrastructure and does not transmit state data to train third-party
models
- Audit logging: Usage tracking for accountability and compliance

Source: City of San Jose, Information Technology Department. Last updated April 24, 2025. Full document: 33 pages.
Executive Summary
Generative AI is a branch of AI technology that interacts with user-provided creativity to generate content - such as memos,
letters, proposals, images, voice, and music. The City of San Jose recognizes the opportunity for a controlled, ethical, and
responsible approach to AI that acknowledges benefits to efficiency while mitigating risks around AI bias, privacy, and
cybersecurity.
Baseline Rules for Generative AI Use
At a baseline, users must follow these rules while using Generative AI for City work or using City resources:
- Rule 1: Information entered into GenAI may be subject to Public Records Act requests, may be viewable by the
company, and may be leaked. Unless using a City-issued tool, users are prohibited from prompts with non-public
information.
- Rule 2: Review, revise, and fact check via multiple sources any content created from GenAI. Users are responsible
for accuracy and appropriateness.
- Rule 3: Limit environmental impact by using search engines for general questions, using fewer prompts where
feasible, and putting boundaries on outputs.
- Rule 4: Always cite images, video, and audio generated by AI. Cite other content generated entirely by AI.
- Rule 5: Record at least one instance of your GenAI use via the City's Generative AI Form.
- Rule 6: Create an account specifically for City use to ensure public records are kept separate from personal
records.
- Rule 7: Departments may provide additional rules. Consult your manager for department-specific rules.
- Rule 8: Refer to this document quarterly, as guidance will change with technology, laws, and best practices.
- Rule 9: Participate in established workgroups to help advance AI usage best practices.
- Rule 10: Attend AI training sessions or review online training conducted by ITD.
Definitions
- User: Staff, contractors, interns/fellows, volunteers using GenAI for City work
- Generative AI: A machine that automatically creates content such as text, audio, or image
- AI: Machines doing tasks that typically require human intelligence
- Machine Learning: A type of AI where computers use data to 'learn' tasks through algorithms
- Algorithm: A set of steps designed to perform a task or solve a problem
Principles for Using Generative AI
Privacy
Submit only information ready for public disclosure. Be mindful that outputs may include unexpected personal information.
Accuracy
Review and fact check all outputs via trustworthy sources including official City documents and peer-reviewed journals.
Transparency
Be clear when using GenAI. Cite AI usage in creating products.
Equity
AI responses may contain historical biases across race, sex, gender identity, ability. Be mindful of stereotypical assumptions.
Accountability
The person using AI is accountable for the content it generates. Level of caution should correspond to risk level.

Any retained conversations relating to City work may be subject to public records requests and must comply with City retention

Copyright
Many tools offer some copyright protections, but users should still verify content doesn't infringe copyrights. AI-generated

Opt out of data collection and model training whenever possible. For ChatGPT: Settings > Data Controls > Improve the model

Citing Generative AI
Cite text from GenAI when: (1) content is entirely AI generated, or (2) the tone reads more like GenAI than the City's voice.
For completely AI-generated content without human review (transcripts, chatbots): 'This text is generated by AI and may
contain errors. Do not use it as an official source.'
For human-reviewed content: 'Text drafted with Generative AI, fact-checked by City of San Jose staff. Sources for facts and
figures are provided as they appear.'
Citations for images/video must be embedded into every frame.
Environmental Impact Guidelines
A single GenAI query uses 6-10x more energy than a traditional web search (2.9 Wh vs. 0.3 Wh). Prompting for 10-50 queries
consumes 500ml of water. By 2030, an estimated 200 TWh (1.6% of total US electricity) will come from AI.
Individual actions to limit impact:
- Use search engines for general questions (add '-ai' to exclude AI results)
- Be thoughtful - disable default AI features when not needed
- Minimize number of prompts - combine queries where possible
- Minimize expected output - specify length constraints like 'respond in less than 100 words'
Risk Assessment Framework
GenAI risk is determined by two factors:
1. Risk of Information Breach
Potential harm if information exchanged with GenAI is released to unintended audience. Low risk: non-identifying,
non-confidential. Mid risk: PII, confidential business info. High risk: credit cards, SSN, bank accounts, protected health
information.
2. Risk of Adverse Impact
Potential harm of using output for decisions, tasks, or services. Consider equity lens for impacts across race, age, gender
identity, disability. Low: internal drafts. Mid: official City documents. High: tasks affecting rights, freedoms, or access to
services.
